METRO DiD SUMMARY
========================================

1) DATA
---------------------------
The cleaned metro panel contains 612 monthly observations from 2009-02 through 2026-02.
The sample covers 3 metro areas: Chicago, Los Angeles, New York.
Each metro contributes Chicago: 204, Los Angeles: 204, New York: 204 observations.

This is a very small panel in the cross-sectional sense because there are only three metros.
That means the regressions can still be estimated, but inference should be treated cautiously because clustering is done at the metro level and there are only three clusters.

2) HOW MUCH TREATMENT
------------------------------
The core treated-month indicator equals 1 in 28 metro-months, which is 4.58% of the full sample.
Treated months by metro:
Chicago: 6 treated months (2017-07, 2018-07, 2019-07, 2020-07, 2024-01, 2025-01)
Los Angeles: 9 treated months (2016-07, 2017-07, 2018-07, 2019-07, 2020-07, 2023-01, 2024-01, 2025-01, 2026-01)
New York: 13 treated months (2009-08, 2014-01, 2015-01, 2016-01, 2017-01, 2018-01, 2019-01, 2021-01, 2022-01, 2023-01, 2024-01, 2025-01, 2026-01)

Across treated months, the average minimum-wage increase is about 7.44%,
the median is about 7.38%,
the smallest is about 1.40%,
and the largest is about 21.21%.

Most months in the data are untreated.
The identifying variation comes from a fairly small number of wage-hike months, and the typical hike is moderate, 
though a few are much larger.

3) MODEL 1: MAIN INTENSITY DID
------------------------------
Dependent variable: monthly log change in FAFH CPI
Treatment regressor: dlog_mw_core_local

Estimate on dlog_mw_core_local: 0.001662
Clustered standard error: 0.008749
t-value: 0.189932
p-value: 0.866893

Implied pass-through:
A 10% minimum-wage increase implies an estimated 1.662% change in FAFH prices.

The point estimate is positive, so the fitted direction goes the way standard pass-through stories would suggest.
But it is extremely small and statistically weak in this sample.
Thus, this model does not provide strong evidence that restaurant prices rose meaningfully in response to the 
wage hikes in this three-metro panel.

4) MODEL 2: BINARY TREATED-MONTH DID
------------------------------------
Dependent variable: monthly log change in FAFH CPI
Treatment regressor: treated_core

Estimate on treated_core: 0.000157
Clustered standard error: 0.000442
t-value: 0.356117
p-value: 0.755810

This version asks: do FAFH prices move differently in months with a wage hike versus months without one?
The estimate is positive but extremely close to zero, and it is not statistically strong.
So this specification also does not show a clear treated-versus-untreated price jump.

5) MODEL 3: DYNAMIC LEAD/CURRENT/LAG DID
----------------------------------------
Lead coefficient: -0.011992  (SE 0.008282, p = 0.284603)
Current coefficient: 0.000424  (SE 0.008336, p = 0.964039)
Lag coefficient: -0.013339  (SE 0.004989, p = 0.116057)

Cumulative lead + current + lag elasticity: -0.024907
Implied pass-through for a 10% minimum-wage increase: -24.907%

The lead coefficient is negative, which could suggest prices were already moving before the policy month, but it is not statistically strong here.
The contemporaneous coefficient is basically zero.
The lag coefficient is also negative and larger in magnitude, though still not conventionally significant in this setup.
Taken together, the dynamic pattern does not look like clean positive pass-through, suggesting instead 
either noisy timing, sparse treatment variation, or measurement/specification limitations in this sample.

6) MODEL 4: SENSITIVITY WITH EQUAL-WEIGHT MULTISTATE TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------
Estimate on dlog_mw_multistate: 0.002011
Clustered standard error: 0.007564
t-value: 0.265850
p-value: 0.815251

Implied pass-through:
A 10% minimum-wage increase implies an estimated 2.011% change in FAFH prices.

This alternative treatment construction gives a result very similar to Model 1.
That is useful because it means the headline result is not being driven only by one way of mapping metro exposure.
But it is still tiny and statistically weak.

7) MODEL 5: ROBUSTNESS WITH METRO FE + YEAR-MONTH FE
----------------------------------------------------
Estimate on dlog_mw_core_local: 0.004825
Clustered standard error: 0.009000
t-value: 0.536141
p-value: 0.645511

Implied pass-through:
A 10% minimum-wage increase implies an estimated 4.825% change in FAFH prices.

This is the toughest specification because year-month fixed effects absorb any shocks common to all metros in the same 
month.
The estimate stays positive, and it becomes somewhat larger than in Model 1, but it is still not statistically strong.
So even after tightening the fixed effects, the evidence for pass-through remains weak.

8) WHAT THE FIT STATISTICS MEAN
-------------------------------
Across models, the adjusted R-squared values are modest and the within R-squared values are very small.
That means the fixed effects explain some background variation, but the treatment variables themselves explain 
very little of the month-to-month within-metro movement in FAFH inflation.

The models are mostly picking up stable metro differences and calendar patterns, which is indeed not a 
strong wage-hike signal.

9) INTERPRETATION
-----------------------------
In this three-metro treated-versus-untreated DID panel, the estimated pass-through from minimum-wage hikes into FAFH CPI is small and imprecise.

More specifically:
- Model 1 suggests about a 1.662% FAFH price increase for a 10% wage increase.
- Model 4 suggests about a 2.011% FAFH price increase for a 10% wage increase.
- Model 5 suggests about a 4.825% FAFH price increase for a 10% wage increase.
- The dynamic model gives a cumulative estimate of -24.907%, which is negative.

The sample does not reproduce the larger positive pass-through magnitudes prev. reported in prior literature.
We see very small effects and substantial uncertainty

10) LIMITATIONS
-------------------------
1. Only three metros are in the panel, so clustered standard errors should be interpreted very cautiously.
2. Only 28 treated metro-months appear in the core treatment variable, so identification is sparse.
3. The coefficients are not conventionally statistically significant in the exported coefficient table.
4. A negative dynamic cumulative estimate does not necessarily mean wage hikes actually lowered prices; 
it can also reflect timing issues, noise, limited sample size, or treatment-mapping problems.

The safest summary is that this metro DID exercise finds weak and statistically inconclusive evidence of 
price pass-through in FAFH CPI over this sample, with point estimates that are much smaller than the 
literature would suggest.
